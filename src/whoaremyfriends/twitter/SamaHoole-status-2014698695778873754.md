Until the late 19th century, all milk was raw. Not by choice. Because pasteurisation didn't exist yet.

Humans drank raw milk for thousands of years. It was the default. Nobody questioned it.

Then industrialisation happened. Dairy farming moved from small local farms to large urban operations. Cows were kept in crowded, unsanitary conditions. Fed industrial waste from distilleries. Disease spread.

The milk from these operations was genuinely dangerous. Contaminated with bacteria from sick cows kept in filth.

Solution: Pasteurise. Heat the milk to kill the bacteria.

This was necessary because industrial dairy was producing contaminated product. The heating process was emergency response to industrial farming practices that created disease.

But once pasteurisation became standard, the narrative shifted. Raw milk became "dangerous" categorically. Not "dangerous when produced in filthy industrial conditions." Just dangerous.

Meanwhile, small dairies producing clean raw milk from healthy cows on pasture continued doing what they'd done for millennia. The milk was safe. Always had been.

But regulation didn't distinguish between industrial contaminated milk and clean farm-fresh milk. All raw milk was banned or heavily restricted.

This served industrial dairy. Small farms producing raw milk were competitors. Making their product illegal eliminated competition.

The safety argument was real for industrial operations. But it got applied universally to destroy traditional dairy farming.

Today, raw milk from clean operations is illegal to sell in many places. Meanwhile, pasteurised milk from industrial dairy with antibiotics, hormones, and corn-fed cows is totally fine.

The irony: Pasteurisation was invented because industrial dairy created disease. Then industrial dairy used pasteurisation requirements to eliminate traditional dairy.

Raw milk from healthy pasture-raised cows is nutritionally superior to pasteurised industrial milk. The heating process destroys enzymes, damages proteins, and reduces vitamin content.

But you can't say this without being labelled anti-science.

Science says pasteurisation is necessary. Science doesn't say "necessary because industrial dairy is filthy," it says "necessary because raw milk is dangerous."

Those aren't the same statement.

Traditional raw milk from clean dairies was safe for thousands of years. It's still safe when produced properly.

Industrial raw milk from crowded, disease-ridden operations was dangerous. Pasteurisation fixed the symptom while allowing the disease to continue.

We could have regulated dairy hygiene. Maintained standards that made raw milk safe.
Instead, we regulated the milk. Made heating mandatory. Eliminated the competition.

Industrial dairy won. Traditional farming lost.

And now people think milk was always pasteurised and that raw milk is inherently dangerous.

It wasn't. It isn't. But the narrative serves industrial interests, so here we are.
