In 1929, a physician at the Mayo Foundation wrote an article called "The Milk Cure."

His name was Dr. J. R. Crewe. He had been running a small sanitarium for sixteen years, treating patients with a single intervention: raw milk from pasture-raised cows. Nothing else.

He treated tuberculosis. Heart disease. Kidney disease. Hypertension. Diabetes. Severe psoriasis. Neurological conditions. Obesity.

He wrote: "The results have been so regularly satisfactory that I have naturally become enthusiastic."

A soldier confined in a government hospital with tuberculosis wrote to say he'd started the milk cure after hearing about it from a former patient. He said several other men in the ward had followed him. "The patients had to buy their own milk," Crewe noted, "and received no encouragement from the hospital authorities."

1929. The institution was already discouraging it.

This didn't become history because it stopped working.

It became history because you can't patent a cow.
