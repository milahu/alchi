John Harvey Kellogg invented cornflakes in 1894.

He invented them specifically to reduce sexual arousal. This is not contested. This is documented. He wrote about it at length, in published texts, with complete conviction. Meat, he believed, stimulated the animal passions. Bland, unseasoned, grain-based foods would calm them. Cornflakes were not a breakfast innovation. They were a libido intervention.

He also invented peanut butter. And granola. And soy-based meat substitutes. And various other products that the modern health food industry has since elevated to icon status, each of which was designed, at its origin, to replace the animal foods he believed were making people degenerate.

He performed circumcisions on boys at the Battle Creek Sanitarium without anaesthetic. He stated explicitly that the pain was the therapeutic mechanism: that suffering would create an aversive association with sexual feeling.

He recommended carbolic acid applications for girls for the same purpose. He did this not as a private aberration but as a published medical protocol, in a celebrated institution, with the endorsement of his professional community.

He championed eugenics with genuine enthusiasm. In 1906 he founded the Race Betterment Foundation and convened three national conferences on the subject of improving the human stock through selective reproduction, immigration restriction, and the management of the "unfit." He corresponded with figures whose racial theories were, in the decades that followed, not considered a good look. He did not seem to notice the problem.

He never shared a bedroom with his wife for the entirety of their forty-one year marriage. He adopted all of his children. Physical intimacy was, in his personal taxonomy, a form of contamination.

He also produced some genuinely useful medical ideas, which is how these things always work. He is not straightforwardly a villain. He is something more uncomfortable: a man whose dietary philosophy was inseparable from his desire to manage, suppress, and improve human beings according to his own criteria of what a proper human being should be.

His cereal is still on the shelf. His ideology is still in the guidelines.

That's the part worth sitting with.
