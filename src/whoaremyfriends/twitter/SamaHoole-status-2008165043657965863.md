1977: Senator George McGovern, who has zero medical training, releases dietary guidelines for 250 million Americans. His chief advisor is journalist Nick Mottern, also with zero nutritional credentials.

Dr. Pete Ahrens from Rockefeller University testifies: "We have no proof that dietary changes will prevent heart disease. The evidence simply doesn't exist yet."

McGovern interrupts: "Senators don't have the luxury of waiting for absolute proof. We must make recommendations now."

Dr. Robert Olson presents data from populations with high saturated fat intake and low heart disease. The committee dismisses this as "interesting but not conclusive."

Ancel Keys' cherry-picked Seven Countries Study? Conclusive enough to base national policy on.

The American Medical Association formally opposes the guidelines: "The evidence is not definitive. We recommend against these sweeping changes."

McGovern ignores them.

The guidelines are released: Reduce saturated fat to 10% of calories. Replace with grains and vegetable oils.

Overnight, butter becomes dangerous. Margarine becomes healthy.

By 1980, these become official US policy.

What happened next:
1980: Obesity 15%
 2000: Obesity 30%
 2020: Obesity 42%

Diabetes increased 600%. Heart disease remained the #1 killer despite everyone following the recommendations.

McGovern died in 2012. The guidelines he created with zero qualification have killed more people than most wars.

But at least he made a decision quickly. Wouldn't want to wait for actual evidence.
