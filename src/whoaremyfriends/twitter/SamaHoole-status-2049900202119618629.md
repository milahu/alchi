From the 1960s through the 1980s, two American chemical companies sold a pesticide called DBCP to banana plantations across Central America. It was sprayed on the soil to kill nematodes. It also, as it turned out, sterilised the men who applied it.

By the late 1970s, even the manufacturers had internal evidence that DBCP was causing sterility in workers in their own factories. They quietly stopped using it on US crops. They kept selling it abroad.

Roughly 20,000 banana plantation workers across Costa Rica, Nicaragua, Honduras, Panama, and the Philippines have spent decades suing Dow and Shell and the fruit companies that bought from them. Many of them are sterile. Many of their wives miscarried repeatedly. Many died in their fifties of cancers consistent with chronic chemical exposure.
