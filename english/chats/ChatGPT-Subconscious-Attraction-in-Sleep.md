## Response:

There *are* human studies on how people move and position themselves during sleep in shared spaces—but not in the sense of a hidden “attraction/rejection force” between bodies or personality-driven physical magnetism.

What *does* exist is a fairly solid research field around **co-sleeping behavior, sleep micro-movements, and interpersonal regulation of distance**. It just ends up looking much more ordinary (comfort, temperature, habit, attachment, arousal state) than instinctive “bonding fields.”

### What researchers actually study

**1. Couples’ sleep coordination (actigraphy / polysomnography)**  
In sleep labs and home studies, researchers track how partners move using motion sensors (actigraphy) or video + EEG.

Findings:

- Long-term partners often show **movement synchrony** (turning, waking, or settling in loosely correlated patterns).
- This is usually explained by **shared environment + light touch + learned habits**, not unconscious attraction forces.
- Sleep disruption in one partner often affects the other (micro-awakenings, repositioning).

But: synchronization is weak and inconsistent, not like a “coupled system” that seeks optimal distance.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**2. Interpersonal distance regulation during sleep**  
There *is* evidence that sleeping humans adjust proximity—but the drivers are pretty grounded:

- Thermoregulation (people move apart when too warm, closer when cold)
- Comfort pressure points
- Mattress space constraints
- Habitual sleeping position patterns
- Sensory sensitivity (movement, snoring, touch aversion)

So what looks like “repulsion” is usually just **discomfort minimization**, not social rejection.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**3. Attachment theory in co-sleeping**  
Some studies connect waking behavior and sleep positioning to attachment styles (secure, anxious, avoidant):

- More securely attached couples may tolerate closer sleep proximity.
- Avoidant individuals may prefer more space.

But this shows up mostly in **conscious preferences and wake-state behavior**, and only weakly in unconscious drifting during sleep.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**4. Sleep in strangers / unfamiliar pairings**  
There are a few controlled studies where strangers share rooms or beds (usually for logistics experiments, space missions analogs, or hotel-style sleep research).

General result:

- People initially maintain **greater distance**
- Over nights, distance may decrease slightly as **familiarity and safety perception increase**
- Movement patterns remain mostly independent

No evidence shows stable “compatibility attraction signatures.”

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**5. Infant / caregiver co-sleeping**  
This is actually the richest area:

- Infants and caregivers show **remarkably coordinated micro-movements and arousal coupling**
- This is strongly tied to survival physiology (breathing regulation, thermal regulation, proximity seeking reflexes)

But again: this is a developmental biological system, not personality-driven attraction between equals.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### What science does *not* support

There is currently **no evidence** for:

- subconscious “attraction vs rejection” forces between arbitrary adult bodies
- personality types producing predictable physical “pull” or “push” in shared sleep
- stable interpersonal “fields” visible as bed movement patterns

Sleep movement is real—but it behaves like a mix of:

> biomechanics + comfort optimization + sensory feedback + learned social habits

not like a hidden social physics.

# 1. What *has* been tested: attachment style + sleep behavior

The closest direct line of research is **attachment theory applied to co-sleeping couples**.

A representative study you just indirectly triggered is:

- couples wore **actigraphy (movement sensors)** for ~10–14 days
- completed **attachment anxiety / avoidance scales**
- sleep quality and synchrony were measured

Key finding pattern across this literature:

- **Attachment anxiety** → more fragmented sleep, more nighttime wake sensitivity
- **Attachment avoidance** → more independent sleep behavior, sometimes more stable sleep alone, but not necessarily more “distance movement”
- **Partner attachment mismatch (e.g., anxious + avoidant)** → worse sleep quality outcomes overall [Sage Journals+1](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0265407520958476?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

But crucial detail:

> These studies measure sleep quality and synchrony much better than *physical drifting distance in bed*

So personality is linked to *sleep quality and co-regulation*, not clean geometric “approach/avoid trajectories.”

# 2. What *has* been measured: sleep synchrony (not “attraction fields”)

There is a solid literature on:

### “sleep concordance” in couples

Researchers compute things like:

- how often both partners are asleep simultaneously
- how often both wake together
- movement correlation across the night

Example work shows:

- measurable **dyadic interdependence in sleep–wake cycles**
- moderate synchrony linked to relationship satisfaction [PubMed+1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25581920/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

But again:

- this is **temporal coupling**, not spatial “pull/push”
- it doesn’t reveal “who moves toward whom,” just that activity patterns correlate

# 4. The key missing piece: *unconscious spatial trajectory modeling*

What you are imagining is something like:

> time-series tracking of 3+ sleepers → infer latent “attraction vs repulsion vectors” based on movement drift

That *has not really been done in personality terms*, because:

### (A) Methodological limitation

Most sleep studies use:

- wrist actigraphy
- pressure mats
- or single-body posture classification

These are not great at:

- mapping fine-grained interpersonal distance continuously in 3D space

### (B) Experimental design limitation

Three-person co-sleeping is rarely studied because:

- it introduces confounds (space constraint dominates behavior)
- ethics/logistics (sleep lab complexity)
- analysis becomes underdetermined (too many interacting constraints)

But:

> awake “personal space preference” ≠ unconscious sleep movement geometry

The mapping between the two is weak.

# 6. Why your intuition is *partly right but mis-aimed*

There *is* something real you’re noticing, but it’s not “attraction fields.”

Instead, sleep proximity dynamics are mostly:

### biological constraints

- heat dissipation
- pressure comfort

- circadian phase alignment

### learned social regulation

- safety signaling
- touch as calming cue (oxytocin-related effects)

### attachment system modulation

- anxious attachment → more monitoring, wake sensitivity
- avoidant attachment → more independence in sleep behavior

So you do get:

> systematic differences in co-sleeping behavior across people

But it emerges from **physiology + learned behavior + emotional regulation**, not hidden interpersonal forces.
