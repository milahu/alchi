## Response:

I get the frustration you’re describing—but it’s worth being careful not to flatten all religious belief into the same thing. Even within Christianity, ideas about the Second Coming range from symbolic/metaphorical to very literal, and only a minority actively frame it in the extreme, destructive way you’re talking about. That said, there *is* a long tradition of satire and dystopian fiction that pokes at apocalyptic thinking, blind faith, and the danger of people trying to “force” destiny.

Here are some works that come pretty close to what you’re looking for—some comedic, some darker:

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Dark satire / critique of apocalyptic belief

- **Good Omens (and its TV adaptation)**  
  This is probably the closest match in tone to Monty Python. It outright mocks end-times obsession: angels and demons themselves try to *stop* the apocalypse because humans have become more interesting than the divine plan. It’s less about believers destroying the world, but very much about the absurdity of prophecy and people misinterpreting it.
- **The Rapture**  
  Much darker and more unsettling. It takes end-times belief seriously on the surface, but ends up feeling like a critique of how such beliefs can distort morality and human connection.
- **This Is the End**  
  A crude comedy, but it plays with the idea of the apocalypse happening in a very selective, almost arbitrary way, poking at who gets “saved” and why.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Dystopian / authoritarian religion

- **The Handmaid’s Tale (and series)**  
  Not about the Second Coming specifically, but very relevant to your “religious people occupy all positions of power” idea. It shows a society where extremist interpretations of religion dominate law, medicine, and politics. The critique is sharp: belief used as justification for control.
- **Parable of the Sower**  
  Features a collapsing society where dangerous belief systems spread, including groups that *welcome* the end. It also has that “minority of clear-sighted thinkers” angle you mentioned.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Satire of blind faith / misinterpretation

- **Life of Brian** by Monty Python  
  You already referenced them, but this one is essential: it directly shows how people project meaning onto someone who clearly *isn’t* a messiah, highlighting exactly your point about expectations overriding reality.
- **Small Gods**  
  This one might hit your idea almost perfectly: a religion becomes powerful and oppressive, but its god is weak because people don’t truly *think*—they just follow. It’s a very sharp critique of institutionalized belief and ignorance.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Bleaker / “humanity destroys itself” vibes

- **Cat's Cradle**  
  Not specifically Christian, but it’s about a ridiculous religion (Bokononism) coexisting with scientific irresponsibility, ultimately leading to global destruction. It nails that mix of satire + apocalypse + human stupidity.
- **Dr. Strangelove**  
  Again, not religious, but very aligned with your “people in power blindly pushing toward total destruction” idea. Replace nuclear doctrine with apocalyptic theology and the structure is similar.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### A note on what you’re looking for

Your exact scenario—fanatics *deliberately* destroying the planet to trigger a messiah who never comes—is actually surprisingly rare as a fully realized plot. Most writers either:

- Treat apocalyptic belief humorously (like *Good Omens*), or
- Show authoritarian religious systems (like *Handmaid’s Tale*), or
- Depict self-destruction without tying it strictly to religion (*Dr. Strangelove*, *Cat’s Cradle*).

The very specific “they ruin everything and think it’s a happy ending” angle does exist in fragments, but not often as a mainstream narrative—possibly because it’s so bleak there’s little room left for character arcs or resolution.

## A reality check on the “kill everyone to reach paradise” idea

Groups like Peoples Temple or Heaven's Gate didn’t usually aim to destroy the *entire world*.  
Their logic was more inward:

- “We are the chosen group”
- “The outside world is corrupt or doomed anyway”
- “Death is escape / transition to something better”

The “take the whole planet with us” idea shows up more in **fictional villains** or **apocalyptic cult thrillers**, because it raises the stakes. In reality, these groups tend to isolate rather than globalize.
