Yes — people *have* studied whether physical properties correlate with personality traits, but the scientific consensus is **very skeptical**, and many classic approaches like physiognomy and morphopsychology are considered **pseudoscience**. Here’s a clear overview of what research *has* looked at and what it *actually* shows:

## 🧠 1. Historical Attempts: Physiognomy & Related Theories

### **Physiognomy & Morphopsychology**

- **Physiognomy** (inferring character from facial features) and **morphopsychology** have existed for centuries and were popular in the 18th–19th centuries (e.g., Lavater, phrenology). [noe.gv.at](https://www.noe.gv.at/noe/Kinderbetreuung/AG145The_Big_Five_ONLINE_1.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- Classic claims (e.g., “high forehead = intelligence”) have **not held up under rigorous scientific testing**. Systematic studies found *no meaningful correlations* between facial and body traits and measured personality or aptitude. [PSYCHOLOGICAL SCALES](https://scales.arabpsychology.com/trm/physiognomy/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- Today these are generally regarded as **pseudoscientific** by mainstream psychology.

## 📊 2. Modern Empirical Research

### **Facial Features & Behavioural Tendencies**

- Some studies have investigated whether metrics like the facial width-to-height ratio (*fWHR*) relate to behaviour (e.g., dominance or aggression).
  - A large study with ~137,000 participants **found no substantial link** between fWHR and self-reported personality scales (including Big Five traits). [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.09979?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

### **Face Shape and Hormones**

- There is preliminary evidence that **prenatal and postnatal sex hormones** (e.g., testosterone) influence both *physical facial structures* and some behaviour traits (e.g., competitiveness/domination). This could create indirect statistical correlations between facial form and behaviour. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-65358-6?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
  - Note: this doesn’t validate physiognomy — it shows hormones can influence *development* of both body and behaviour, not that a face *predicts* personality reliably.

### **Vision-based Personality Analysis**

- In computer vision research, algorithms can predict *perceived* personality traits from faces and postures — but this is about **observer judgments**, not ground-truth personality. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.08046?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

## 🧬 3. Body Shape, Hormones, and Traits

### **Body Shape & Socio-economic Outcomes**

- Machine learning studies have linked aspects of body shape (from 3D scans) with outcomes like **income**, which may correlate indirectly with personality or behaviour. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.06747?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

### **Constitutional Theories (Classic)**

- Early constitutional psychology attempted to link body types (e.g., Kretschmer’s or Sheldon’s somatotypes) with psychiatric outcomes — but these findings were confounded by age and other factors and didn’t reliably map to personality. [Studocu](https://www.studocu.com/de/document/friedrich-schiller-universitat-jena/personlichkeits-oder-differentielle-psychologie-i/vl-11-physische-merkmale/1873464?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

## 🤝 4. Perception vs. Reality

One important theme in research is that people *tend* to infer personality from appearance — and those inferences can be stable and predictable — **even when they’re not scientifically valid**:

- People form personality judgments from facial features (e.g., dominance, trustworthiness) quickly and consistently. [Wikipedia](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erster_Eindruck?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- But such perceptions **don’t necessarily track actual measured personality traits**. This is a social-cognitive effect, not evidence that physical traits determine personality.

## 🧪 5. Personality & Biological Factors

While physical morphology itself isn’t a reliable predictor, **biology does connect with personality** in other ways that aren’t reflected in gross physical measures:

- **Genetics and brain structure** are known to relate to personality factor variation. [Wikipedia](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Five_%28Psychologie%29?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- Hormonal influences (e.g., testosterone affecting behaviour) are studied in personality biology — but these connections are complex and mediated by many factors.

## 📉 6. What Science Says Overall

**Strong evidence?**  
➡️ **No:** There’s *little to no robust scientific evidence* that static physical traits (face shape, finger length, ear shape, etc.) reliably predict personality traits in the modern psychological sense.

**Moderate evidence?**  
➡️ **Some** associations exist between hormones and behaviour, and humans *perceive* personality from appearance, but these are subtle and not direct mappings of physical structure to personality.

**Pseudoscience warning:**  
➡️ Physiognomy, as historically practiced, is discredited. Modern research rejects its fundamental claim that personality can be read from physical traits alone. [PSYCHOLOGICAL SCALES](https://scales.arabpsychology.com/trm/physiognomy/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
