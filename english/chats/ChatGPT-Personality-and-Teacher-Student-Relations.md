Yes. There is actually a substantial research literature on **teacher-student relationship quality and personality**, although it does not generally support a strong deterministic model. What it does support is a weaker claim that is surprisingly close to parts of your Pallas hypothesis:

> The quality of a relationship depends not only on the teacher's personality and not only on the student's personality, but on the interaction between the two.

This is often called a **person-environment fit**, **dyadic fit**, or **interpersonal complementarity** perspective.

Some relevant findings:

### 1. Student personality predicts teacher-student relationship quality

Research using the Big Five repeatedly finds that students who are:

- high in agreeableness,
- high in conscientiousness,
- low in antagonism,

tend to have warmer relationships with teachers and receive more positive evaluations.

Students high in aggression, oppositionality, or antagonism tend to experience more conflict.

This already suggests that trust and influence are not distributed randomly.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2. Teacher personality also matters

Teachers high in:

- agreeableness,
- emotional stability,
- extraversion,

typically report better relationships with students.

Teachers high in neuroticism tend to report more conflict.

So relationship quality emerges from both sides.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### 3. The strongest evidence for your idea comes from interpersonal complementarity theory

A major framework in personality psychology is the **Interpersonal Circumplex**, developed by researchers such as Timothy Leary, Jerry Wiggins, and later others.

The model uses two dimensions:

- Dominance ↔ Submission
- Warmth ↔ Hostility

One of its central observations:

- Dominance tends to invite submission.
- Submission tends to invite dominance.
- Warmth tends to invite warmth.
- Hostility tends to invite hostility.

This is called **interpersonal complementarity**.

Your teacher/student gradient resembles the dominance/submission axis.

A prediction from complementarity theory would be:

- Some teacher-student pairings naturally stabilize.
- Other pairings naturally generate friction.
- Attempts to reverse the expected influence pattern may create resistance.

This is already an established idea, though usually framed probabilistically rather than deterministically.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### 4. Leader-follower research finds similar effects

In organizational psychology, there is extensive work on leader-follower relationships.

For example:

- followers differ in willingness to accept influence,
- leaders differ in willingness to receive feedback,
- the quality of the leader-member relationship depends partly on personality compatibility.

The concept of **Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)** argues that leaders do not relate identically to every subordinate; instead, each relationship forms its own trust/influence structure.

This sounds remarkably close to your proposal that social roles emerge from specific pairings rather than from fixed "leader" and "follower" identities.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### 5. Where your hypothesis goes beyond current research

The most distinctive claim I see is:

> Personality combinations automatically generate an asymmetric trust/influence gradient.

Most personality research would currently consider this too strong.

Researchers generally find:

- personality predicts tendencies,
- interaction patterns become self-reinforcing,
- expectations can create stable hierarchies,

but they stop short of saying the hierarchy is predetermined.

Your example:

> Student notices teacher's error → teacher rejects correction because the teacher instinctively distrusts that student.

Psychologists would probably interpret this through several mechanisms:

- status dynamics,
- dominance hierarchies,
- expectancy effects,
- ego threat,
- interpersonal complementarity,

rather than assuming a fixed personality-type law.

However, empirically, the phenomenon itself is very plausible. People often accept criticism differently depending on *who* delivers it. The same correction can be accepted from one person and rejected from another.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### An interesting way to formalize the Pallas hypothesis

If I were translating your idea into a research model, I would state it as:

> For any two individuals A and B, personality traits generate a latent trust-influence matrix.
>
> The matrix predicts:
>
> - who tends to influence whom,
> - who trusts whom,
> - whether advice is accepted or rejected,
> - whether leadership roles emerge,
> - and whether role reversals create conflict.

That formulation is actually testable.

You could imagine measuring:

- Big Five traits,
- interpersonal circumplex coordinates,
- perceived influence,
- perceived trust,

and then checking whether certain personality pairings consistently produce asymmetric influence structures.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### One thing that stands out

Your idea that people are neither inherently leaders nor followers, but rather:

> leader with some people, follower with others,

is much closer to modern psychology than the traditional "born leader" model.

In fact, social psychology increasingly treats status and influence as **relational properties**, not fixed individual properties.

A useful comparison might be:

- Traditional theory: "John is dominant."
- Pallas hypothesis: "John is dominant relative to Alice but submissive relative to Maria."

That relational formulation is theoretically stronger and, in many ways, more compatible with contemporary findings.

So I would say that **parts of your hypothesis are already supported by interpersonal complementarity, leader-member exchange theory, and teacher-student relationship research**, but the stronger claim that personality pairings *predetermine* trust and influence gradients remains largely untested and would require new empirical evidence.

On the scientific side, there are several researchers whose work may be surprisingly close to your relational perspective.

### Interpersonal complementarity

I would start with:

- Jerry Wiggins
- Donald Kiesler
- Timothy Leary

Kiesler in particular emphasized that interpersonal behavior tends to evoke predictable responses from others.

For example:

- dominance tends to elicit submission,
- submission tends to elicit dominance,
- warmth tends to elicit warmth,
- hostility tends to elicit hostility.

Notice how this is already relational. A person's behavior is not treated as an isolated property but as part of a feedback loop.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Relational models of personality

Another interesting figure is:

- David Funder

Funder argued that personality expression depends strongly on situations and observers. A trait is not merely something inside a person; it is also reflected in how others respond to them.

This is still not as dyadic as your theory, but it moves away from the simplistic "John is dominant everywhere" model.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Social Relations Model

You may find this especially interesting:

- David Kenny

Kenny developed the Social Relations Model (SRM).

SRM decomposes relationship outcomes into:

1.  actor effects (what I generally do),
2.  partner effects (what others generally do),
3.  relationship effects (what happens specifically between us).

That third component is important.

Suppose:

- Alice trusts almost everyone.
- Bob is trusted by almost everyone.

Those are actor and partner effects.

But SRM additionally asks:

> Is there something unique about Alice and Bob together?

That is remarkably close to your idea that the pair itself has properties that neither individual possesses alone.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)

For leadership research:

- George Graen
- Mary Uhl-Bien

LMX theory argues that leaders form different relationships with different followers.

The same manager may be:

- highly trusting toward one employee,
- distrustful toward another,
- receptive to feedback from one,
- defensive toward another.

The theory does not assume a fixed "leader essence." Instead, leadership emerges within specific relationships.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Relational Sociology

Outside psychology, you may like:

- Harrison White
- Mustafa Emirbayer

Emirbayer's famous paper *Manifesto for a Relational Sociology* argues that social science often over-focuses on individuals and under-focuses on relationships.

His basic idea is:

> Relations come first; individuals are partly defined through relations.

That is philosophically quite compatible with your statement that people become more "teacher-like" or "student-like" depending on whom they are interacting with.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Where I think your theory differs from mainstream psychology

The strongest and most unusual part of your hypothesis is not:

> personality matters for relationships

or even

> personality combinations matter.

Most psychologists would already agree with those.

The unusual part is:

> the partner's personality type is the primary mechanism that switches a person between active/passive, leading/following, influencing/trusting modes.

That is a much stronger claim.

Mainstream psychology would usually expect subtype switching to depend on multiple factors simultaneously:

- partner characteristics,
- status differences,
- incentives,
- social norms,
- situational demands,
- group structure,
- past relationship history.

Your model appears to elevate the partner's type to the dominant causal factor.

That is a clean and testable prediction, which is actually a strength scientifically. A theory becomes testable precisely when it makes strong predictions that could turn out to be wrong.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### One methodological caution

You mentioned EEG and electrodermal activity.

Those measures could be useful, but they would not directly measure "personality type" or "relationship quality."

EEG and EDA are usually closer to:

- arousal,
- attention,
- stress,
- emotional activation.

To test your theory, researchers would probably combine:

- personality measures,
- trust ratings,
- influence measures,
- behavioral observations,
- physiological measures (EDA, EEG).

Otherwise there is a risk of interpreting physiological arousal as evidence for a specific interpersonal structure when several explanations remain possible.

But the core idea that the **dyad** (the pair) is the fundamental unit of analysis rather than the individual-that is not radical at all. In fact, some of the most interesting work in personality, relationship science, leadership research, and relational sociology has been moving in exactly that direction for decades. The part that remains controversial is whether a small number of personality types can reliably predict those dyadic structures with high accuracy. That's the empirical question your hypothesis would need to answer.
