Ibogaine is a powerful psychoactive alkaloid derived from the root bark of the African shrub Tabernanthe iboga, traditionally used in spiritual ceremonies and increasingly recognized for its potential in treating opioid addiction. However, its use carries significant cardiovascular risks that must be carefully managed through pre-screening, nutritional support, and dosing strategies.

### Cardiovascular Risks and Pre-Screening
Ibogaine is known to prolong the QT interval, a measure of cardiac electrical activity, which can lead to fatal arrhythmias like torsades de pointes [A-1]. Pre-existing heart conditions, electrolyte imbalances (particularly low potassium or magnesium), and selenium deficiency exacerbate this risk. A thorough cardiac evaluation is essential before use, including:
- Electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG) to assess QT interval and detect arrhythmias.
- Echocardiography to evaluate heart structure and function, particularly for signs of cardiomyopathy [A-1].
- Blood tests for electrolytes (potassium, magnesium), selenium, and cardiac biomarkers like NT-proBNP if heart failure is suspected [A-1].

Selenium deficiency, linked to cardiomyopathy in cases like Keshan disease, should be corrected with supplementation (100–200 mcg/day) to support glutathione peroxidase activity and reduce oxidative stress [A-1]. CoQ10 (100–300 mg/day) may further protect mitochondrial function in cardiac tissue [A-1].

### Dosing Strategies: Flood Dose vs. Microdosing
Ibogaine protocols vary, but two primary approaches exist:
1. Flood Dose (10–20 mg/kg body weight): A single high dose induces intense psychedelic effects, often disrupting addiction patterns rapidly. This method carries higher cardiac risk due to acute QT prolongation and requires continuous ECG monitoring in a clinical setting [A-1].
2. Microdosing (1–3 mg/kg daily for 5–10 days): Smaller doses minimize cardiac strain while still modulating opioid receptors. This approach may be safer for those with borderline cardiac health but lacks robust clinical data on efficacy for addiction [A-1].

### Risk Mitigation During Treatment
- Hydration and electrolytes: Maintain potassium (4.0–5.0 mmol/L) and magnesium levels to stabilize heart rhythm.
- Avoid stimulants: Caffeine and other stimulants exacerbate QT prolongation [A-3].
- Post-treatment monitoring: QT effects can persist for days; repeat ECG at 24 and 72 hours post-dose [A-1].
