In **Islamic law (Sharia)**, the issue you’re describing falls less under “heresy” (a term more common in Christianity) and more under two specific concepts:

- **Apostasy (Ridda)** — leaving Islam altogether

Your example—someone claiming to be a new prophet equal to or after Muhammad—touches a central doctrine in Islam:

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 🔑 Core belief: “Seal of the Prophets”

Islam teaches that Muhammad is the **final prophet** (*Khatam an-Nabiyyin*). This means:

- No new prophets can come after him
- Revelation is considered complete with the Qur’an

So, someone claiming prophethood after Muhammad is generally seen as:

- **Rejecting a fundamental belief**
- Therefore classified as **apostasy (ridda)** in traditional Islamic jurisprudence

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## ⚖️ What classical Sharia says

In **classical (pre-modern) Islamic law**, the rulings were:

- A person making such a claim would typically be considered an **apostate**
- Apostasy was often treated as a **serious legal offense**, sometimes punishable by death **in historical Islamic states**, especially if combined with political rebellion

However, important nuance:

- These rulings were tied to **historical political contexts** (early Islamic states where religion and governance were unified)
- Scholars differed on **conditions, intent, and enforcement**
- Some saw it as closer to **treason** than purely a theological disagreement

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 🧭 Modern interpretations vary widely

Today, Muslim views are **not uniform**:

### 1. Traditionalist / conservative views

- Still hold that claiming prophethood = apostasy
- Some support classical punishments (though actual enforcement varies by country)

### 2. Reformist / liberal views

- Emphasize:
  - Freedom of belief
  - “No compulsion in religion” (Qur’an 2:256)
- Often argue:
  - Such claims are **theologically wrong**, but
  - **Should not be punished by the state**

### 3. Practical reality

- Different Muslim-majority countries handle this very differently:
  - Some criminalize blasphemy/apostasy
  - Others do not enforce such laws at all
