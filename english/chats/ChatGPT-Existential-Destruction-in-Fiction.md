## Prompt:

## Response:

Yes — that pattern shows up a lot in tragedy, anti-war fiction, dystopia, horror, mythology, and philosophy. Usually not *exactly* in the form you describe, but many stories orbit the same core idea:

> the attempt to exterminate “evil,” “chaos,” “negativity,” or “the Other” becomes so obsessive and totalizing that it creates the very destruction it claims to prevent.

And many of them end with some version of:  
“Was any of this worth it?”

Here are the closest matches I can think of.

## Most directly aligned with your idea

### Princess Mononoke

This is probably one of the closest spiritually.

Humans expand aggressively, industrialize, and try to dominate nature and the gods/spirits they perceive as hostile or irrational. The “monsters” become more violent *because* humans wage war on them. Hatred literally turns beings into demons. Every escalation creates more corruption.

The film’s central insight is:

- neither side is purely good,
- attempts at domination poison everyone,
- humans refuse coexistence until catastrophe arrives.

It does *not* end with total annihilation, but it absolutely carries the:

> “You could have shared the world.”

energy.

### The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas

A different angle, but philosophically adjacent.

A perfect, “positive,” happy society depends entirely on the suffering of one abused child. The “good” world is structurally built on hidden negativity. The story asks:

- can purity exist without victims?
- is “utopia” just organized cruelty?
- what happens when people rationalize suffering for a greater good?

Le Guin’s work often attacks the idea that goodness can be engineered through domination or exclusion.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Akira

Human attempts to control danger through military escalation, suppression, experimentation, and authoritarian order end up unleashing apocalyptic destruction.

The state keeps trying to eliminate instability by increasing force, surveillance, and control — which amplifies instability further.

Very much:

> the cure becomes worse than the disease.

### Blood Meridian

Not supernatural in the same way, but philosophically brutal.

Civilization claims to destroy savagery while actually reproducing and industrializing it. Violence feeds violence until morality collapses entirely. Judge Holden in particular embodies the idea that humans rationalize domination after the fact, without real reasons beyond will, greed, and power.

The book repeatedly suggests:

- war perpetuates itself,
- humans mythologize their cruelty,
- extermination becomes a sacred project.

## Stories where “purity” destroys the world

### Snowpiercer

Humanity tries to “fix” climate catastrophe through technological intervention and accidentally freezes the world.

Then the surviving social order becomes obsessed with maintaining “balance” through brutality. The system perpetuates suffering to preserve itself.

The whole story is basically:

> attempts to impose total order create permanent horror.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Neon Genesis Evangelion

Especially toward the ending and in The End of Evangelion.

Humanity tries to eliminate pain, loneliness, conflict, and separation itself — to create total unity and transcendence. The result is psychological and existential collapse.

A major recurring idea:

- fear of suffering drives humanity insane,
- attempts to abolish negativity abolish individuality and life itself.

King Pentheus attempts to suppress irrationality, ecstasy, chaos, and Dionysian forces entirely through rigid control and repression.

Very close to:

> denying one half of reality makes it return in monstrous form.

## The single closest thematic sentence I know

The idea you describe reminds me strongly of this line from Princess Mononoke:

> “To see with eyes unclouded by hate.”

Because your scenario is fundamentally about:

- inability to tolerate coexistence,
- moral absolutism,
- self-destructive purification,
- escalation loops,
- hatred becoming autonomous.

The “negative side” in your version almost sounds like the repressed half of existence itself saying:

> “You declared war on reality.”

That exact structure also appears in:

- psychoanalytic thought (repression creates neurosis),
- political extremism,
- ecological collapse,
- religious puritanism,
- revolutionary terror,
- genocidal ideology.

You might also enjoy works by Ursula K. Le Guin, Hayao Miyazaki, and Cormac McCarthy — they repeatedly explore the tragedy of humans trying to dominate rather than coexist.

## Prompt:

You’re describing a very old narrative inversion structure:

> the official moral order is revealed as corrupt, and the “criminal” or “outsider” becomes the only actor with authentic ethics, while institutions become self-legitimizing systems of power.

That pattern shows up a lot, but it comes in *different ideological flavors*, so I’ll group it by type rather than assume one interpretation is “correct.”

------------------------------------------------------------------------

1)  Corporate / state corruption vs “criminals with ethics”  
    ==========================================================

## Robin Hood

The archetype: redistribution vs institutional legitimacy.

Robin Hood is literally:

- labeled criminal by the state,
- but framed as restoring moral balance by redistributing wealth.

Across versions, the tension is:

> legality ≠ legitimacy

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Serpico

A police officer exposes corruption inside law enforcement.

Core idea:

- “law” is used selectively,
- internal corruption is normalized,
- whistleblowing becomes socially punished.

This is one of the clearest “system hides crime within itself” narratives.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Insider

Tobacco industry whistleblower story.

Shows:

- corporate legality vs public harm,
- institutional pressure to suppress truth,
- reputational control over factual truth.

The “criminality” is systemic, not street-level.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Erin Brockovich

Corporation pollutes water while maintaining legal cover.

The “criminal” is:

- bureaucratic,
- dispersed,
- protected by procedure.

The “outsider” becomes the ethical force.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

2)  Police / state brutality vs “criminalized innocents”  
    =======================================================

## The Lives of Others

You already mentioned this, but it fits perfectly.

The state:

- surveils artists and intellectuals,
- treats thought as threat,
- enforces ideological conformity.

Meanwhile:

- “criminality” = independent thought, not harm.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Brazil

One of the purest versions of bureaucratic inversion.

Minor errors become:

- terrorism cases,
- torture justification,
- endless escalation.

The “system” becomes indistinguishable from absurd violence.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Trial

Again central.

Accused person is never told the crime.  
The system is:

- self-justifying,
- unaccountable,
- structurally incomprehensible.

“Guilt” is assigned by procedure, not act.

# The deeper structure behind all these stories

What you’re circling is not just “good vs evil inversion,” but a more precise structural claim:

### 1. Legality ≠ morality

What is lawful is often what preserves the system, not what reduces harm.

### 2. Visibility ≠ reality

Street crime is highly visible; structural harm is diffuse and normalized.

### 3. Systems criminalize threats to their coherence

Not necessarily threats to human well-being.

### 4. Moral language is often post-hoc justification

Power first, narrative second.

### 5. “Good guys” can be system agents

Even when they believe they are moral.
