When people talk about "tolerance to changes in diet," there are actually several different things that can mean:

1.  **Can the animal stay healthy when the feed ingredients change?**
2.  **Can the animal digest a wide variety of foods?**
3.  **How quickly can the diet be changed without causing digestive problems?**
4.  **How much does the animal's meat, milk, or body composition reflect what it ate?**

Different animals rank differently depending on which definition you use.

## Are ruminants more tolerant of dietary changes?

Surprisingly, **not necessarily**.

Ruminants such as Cattle, Sheep, and Goat can digest an enormous variety of feeds because their rumen hosts a complex microbial ecosystem.

They are extremely good at:

- Digesting cellulose and fiber.
- Extracting energy from low-quality plant material.
- Converting non-protein nitrogen into microbial protein.
- Producing relatively consistent meat and milk from variable forage.

However, the rumen ecosystem is delicate. **Abrupt dietary changes can be dangerous.**

Examples:

- Switching suddenly from hay to large amounts of grain can cause ruminal acidosis.
- Rapid changes can disrupt microbial populations.
- Some feeds require gradual adaptation over days or weeks.

So ruminants are often **more flexible in what they can ultimately eat**, but **less tolerant of sudden diet transitions**.

## Monogastrics: less flexible, often more tolerant of transitions

Animals such as Pig, Chicken, and humans have digestive systems that depend less on a giant fermentation chamber.

They generally:

- Cannot utilize cellulose nearly as well.
- Require higher-quality feed.
- Are more limited in the range of feeds they can efficiently convert.

But moderate formulation changes are often easier because there is no rumen microbial ecosystem to destabilize.

That said, young pigs and poultry can still experience digestive upset when feed changes are too abrupt.

## Horses are a special case

Horse is not a true monogastric in the practical sense.

Horses are **hindgut fermenters**:

- Fermentation occurs in the cecum and colon rather than before the stomach.
- They can digest substantial fiber.
- They rely on microbial populations too.

Like ruminants, horses often do poorly with sudden dietary changes and can develop colic or laminitis if concentrates are introduced too rapidly.

Many livestock nutritionists actually consider horses among the animals that require the most gradual feed transitions.

## Which animals are the most diet-flexible overall?

If by "can eat the widest variety of foods and survive" you mean ecological flexibility, omnivores tend to dominate:

- Pig
- Brown Rat
- Raccoon
- Goat

Goats are particularly famous because they can utilize many browse species and roughages that cattle avoid.

If by "can convert poor-quality feed into useful nutrients" you mean agricultural flexibility, then ruminants are usually the champions.

## Your observation about meat quality

There is a lot of truth in what you're describing.

### Monogastrics tend to reflect dietary fat

In pigs and chickens:

- Dietary fatty acids are incorporated relatively directly into body fat.
- Feeding high-polyunsaturated-fat diets generally produces softer, more unsaturated fat.
- Feeding saturated fats produces firmer fat.

So the animal's tissue composition often mirrors the feed.

### Ruminants modify nutrients extensively

In ruminants, rumen microbes transform many nutrients before the animal absorbs them.

One famous example is **biohydrogenation**:

- Unsaturated fatty acids entering the rumen are extensively hydrogenated by microbes.
- Many are converted into more saturated fatty acids before absorption.

As a result:

- Beef and lamb fat tends to be more saturated than pork or chicken fat.
- The fatty acid profile is less directly tied to the original feed.
- Ruminants can "buffer" dietary variation.

This is essentially the "upcycling" effect you're referring to.

## Is ruminant meat inherently higher quality?

That depends on the definition of quality.

From a nutrient-conversion perspective:

- Ruminants are remarkable because they can convert grass, crop residues, and other human-inedible biomass into meat and milk.

From a fatty-acid-stability perspective:

- Beef and lamb fat is generally more saturated and oxidatively stable than pork or chicken fat.

From an efficiency perspective:

- Monogastrics usually convert feed to meat more efficiently.
- Pigs and chickens require less feed per kilogram of meat produced.

From a protein-quality perspective:

- All major livestock species provide high-quality complete protein.

So it would be more accurate to say:

> Ruminants are generally better at transforming low-quality or highly variable feeds into relatively consistent animal products, whereas monogastrics tend to transfer more of the dietary nutrient profile directly into their tissues.

That distinction is one of the major biological differences between the two groups.

In the scenario you're describing, I would generally expect **ruminants to have a major advantage over most monogastric livestock**, but not necessarily because they tolerate abrupt diet changes better. Rather, it's because they are much better equipped to utilize **high-fiber, lower-quality, and often chemically unusual halophytic (salt-tolerant) plants**.

A few distinctions matter:

## 1. The biggest issue is often the salt, not the plant species

Plants such as:

- Saltgrass
- Smooth Cordgrass
- Seablite
- Alkali Sacaton

often contain:

- much higher sodium concentrations,
- higher ash/mineral content,
- various secondary plant compounds,
- sometimes lower digestible energy than conventional pasture grasses.

An animal must handle both the **fiber** and the **salt load**.

## 2. Ruminants are usually superior at utilizing halophytes

The rumen is essentially a microbial bioreactor.

Microbes can:

- break down structural carbohydrates,
- detoxify some plant compounds,
- adapt over time to unusual forage species.

This is one reason goats, sheep, and many wild antelope thrive in semi-arid and saline environments where horses or pigs would struggle.

Among domestic livestock, I would roughly rank adaptability to saline forage something like:

1.  Goats
2.  Sheep
3.  Cattle
4.  Horses
5.  Pigs
6.  Poultry

This is a broad generalization, but it captures the overall pattern.

## 3. Many naturally saline ecosystems are dominated by ruminants

If you look at coastal marshes, salt steppes, semi-deserts, and saline rangelands around the world, the dominant large herbivores are often ruminants:

- Sheep
- Goat
- Dromedary Camel
- Bactrian Camel
- various antelope species

Camels are particularly remarkable because they combine foregut fermentation (similar in function to ruminants) with exceptional salt and water tolerance.

## 4. Horses are less advantaged than many people assume

Although horses can digest fiber via hindgut fermentation, they have limitations:

- less ability to extract energy from very poor-quality forage,
- greater sensitivity to some plant toxins,
- less capacity to convert marginal vegetation into usable nutrition.

A horse can graze certain saline pastures, but if most vegetation shifts toward tough halophytes, sheep, goats, and camels usually outperform horses.

## 5. Pigs and chickens would be the biggest losers

Pigs are biologically omnivores, which makes them flexible in one sense, but not in the sense of grazing salt-marsh plants.

They are poor converters of:

- highly fibrous forage,
- cellulose-rich vegetation,
- coarse halophytes.

Chickens are even less capable of living primarily on such vegetation.

A world where most pasture became salt-marsh vegetation would likely shift livestock production away from poultry and pigs unless substantial feed cropping remained possible elsewhere.

## 6. There is an interesting limit: extreme salinity

Even ruminants have limits.

Some halophytes accumulate so much salt that:

- water requirements increase,
- animal performance declines,
- weight gain slows,
- milk production can fall.

In practice, saline-rangeland systems often work best when animals have access to:

- halophytes,
- less saline companion plants,
- adequate fresh water.

Fresh water becomes especially important because the animals must excrete the excess sodium.

## Evolutionarily speaking

If a giant flood permanently salinized large areas of former grassland, I would expect livestock systems to move toward animals that already dominate marginal and saline environments:

- goats,
- sheep,
- camels,
- certain hardy cattle breeds,

rather than horses, pigs, or poultry.

So your intuition is largely correct: **ruminants would generally handle the transition from conventional freshwater pasture to salt-tolerant pasture far better than most monogastric livestock.** The key advantage is not that they are immune to dietary change, but that their fermentation system allows them to extract nutrition from the kinds of fibrous, salty plants that would replace conventional grasses after widespread salinization.
