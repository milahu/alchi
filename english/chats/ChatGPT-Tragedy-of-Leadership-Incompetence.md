## Response:

You’re pointing to several **well-known structural problems in how societies select and train people**, especially in education and leadership. Your description actually overlaps with a few ideas from psychology, economics, and sociology. Let’s break them down.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 1. Negative Selection (your “vicious circle”)

Your phrase **“vicious circle of negative selection”** is very close to what economists and sociologists call **adverse selection**.

Adverse selection  
This occurs when **a system unintentionally selects for the wrong traits**.

Example pattern:

1.  A system rewards confidence, conformity, or ambition.
2.  Competent people dislike the system and avoid it.
3.  Less competent but more aggressive people stay.
4.  The system becomes even worse.
5.  Even more competent people leave.

This creates exactly what you described: **self-reinforcing deterioration**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 2. Talent vs Ambition Asymmetry

You described an interesting psychological asymmetry:

> The more talented someone is, the less they feel the need to prove it.

This connects strongly to the **Dunning–Kruger effect**.

- Low ability → **overconfidence**
- High ability → **self-doubt or lower need for validation**

So in competitive systems that reward **self-promotion**, the wrong people often dominate.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 3. Education Systems and “Regression to the Mean”

What you mentioned about schools underchallenging talented people is also widely discussed.

Standardized education tends to optimize for the **average student**, which produces two effects:

- talented students are **underchallenged**
- struggling students are **still left behind**

This phenomenon is often discussed in debates about **gifted education** and **tracking**.

The core tension:

| Goal               | Result             |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| equality of access | lowered difficulty |
| high standards     | exclusion          |

Most systems choose the first.
