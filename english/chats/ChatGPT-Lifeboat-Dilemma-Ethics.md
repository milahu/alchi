## Response:

What you’re describing is a classic kind of **lifeboat dilemma**—and you’re far from the first person to frame it that starkly.

### Closely related ideas and stories

- **Lifeboat Ethics (1974)**  
  Hardin explicitly uses the metaphor of a crowded lifeboat with limited capacity. He argues that helping everyone can sink the boat, while refusing help preserves those already inside. It’s controversial because it seems to justify letting others die.
- **The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas**  
  A society’s happiness depends on the suffering of one child. It flips your scenario: instead of many dying for a few, one suffers for many—and asks whether that trade-off is acceptable.
- **The Cold Equations**  
  A spaceship has strict fuel limits; the presence of an extra person means everyone will die unless she is sacrificed. It’s one of the most direct fictional parallels to your setup.
- **Thomas Malthus’s population theory**  
  Malthus argued that population growth can outstrip resources, leading to famine and collapse unless checked—an early version of your “too many people, not enough supplies” framing.
- **The Population Bomb (1968)**  
  A modern echo of Malthusian fears, predicting mass starvation from overpopulation (many predictions didn’t pan out, but the ethical anxiety remains influential).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### What this dilemma is usually called

There isn’t just one name, but several overlapping concepts:

- **Lifeboat ethics** (the closest direct label)
- **Malthusian catastrophe / Malthusian dilemma**
- **Tragic choice** (a situation where every option violates a moral value)
- **Utilitarian sacrifice problem** (maximize survival vs. protect individual rights)
- A variant of the **Trolley Problem**, but with scale and certainty

Your phrasing—*“short life for many vs. long life for few”*—is actually quite close to how philosophers frame it: **total welfare vs. distribution of welfare**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### A key pushback on the premise

Your scenario assumes **certainty**: that doing nothing guarantees everyone dies, and killing 95% guarantees survival of the rest. Real-world versions almost never have that clarity.

That uncertainty is why most ethical systems resist the “kill some to save others” move:

- **Rights-based ethics**: you’re not allowed to intentionally kill innocent people, even for a good outcome.
- **Rule utilitarianism**: if we allow such rules, they can justify atrocities in less clear situations.
- **Practical reality**: once coercion and violence start, outcomes often get worse, not better.

So philosophers often treat your scenario as a **boundary test**, not a policy recommendation.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### On the “active vs. passive” framing

You put it sharply: *active killing vs. passive letting die*. That distinction matters a lot in ethics:

- Many people intuitively see **killing** as worse than **letting die**, even if the outcome is numerically worse.
- Others argue outcomes are what matter most (a more utilitarian view).

This tension is at the heart of debates in medicine, war, and disaster response.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### About the nihilism angle

It’s true that on a cosmic timescale, human life is temporary. But that doesn’t automatically make present choices meaningless. Most ethical systems operate on a **human timescale**—because that’s where suffering, wellbeing, and decisions actually occur.

If anything, these dilemmas feel intense *because* we care about what happens in that limited window.

## About your “risk-aversion paradox”

What you’re describing overlaps with real ideas:

- **Omission bias**: people judge harmful *actions* as worse than equally harmful *inactions*.
- **Precautionary principle**: avoid actions with potentially catastrophic consequences—even if inaction also has risks.
- **Tragic trade-offs**: situations where every option violates a core value (life, fairness, autonomy).

But again, the tricky part is that your framing assumes:

> small risk of killing some vs. large risk of killing all

In fiction—and especially in reality—that often turns into:

> uncertain, contested predictions + irreversible decisions about who dies

That’s where “cold rationality” stops being clean math and becomes politics, trust, and legitimacy.

## Response:

What you’re describing has a long lineage. People have repeatedly imagined a world where **many different social systems can be tried in parallel**, judged by their outcomes rather than argued about in theory. But the interesting part is that most authors who explore this idea also run into the same hard questions: containment, consent, exit rights, and unintended spillovers.

Here are some thinkers and stories that come close to your “many small experiments” model:

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Explicit visions of parallel societies / experimental communities

- **The Dispossessed**  
  Two neighboring societies with radically different systems evolve side by side. It’s not many micro-experiments, but it captures the idea that **you can only judge systems by lived reality over time**, not theory alone.
- **Too Like the Lightning**  
  The world is divided into “Hives”—voluntary global societies with different ideologies. People choose which system to live under, creating a kind of **parallel governance experiment** at scale.
- **Walkaway**  
  Groups literally “walk away” from mainstream society and build alternative communities. It leans strongly into the idea that **experimentation should be cheap and reversible**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Political / philosophical versions of your idea

- **Robert Nozick – *“utopia of utopias”***  
  In *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, he proposes a framework where many different communities with different rules coexist, and people can move between them. This is probably the closest formal philosophical match to your idea.
- **Charles Tiebout – “voting with your feet”**  
  Not fiction, but influential: people choose between communities with different policies, and competition reveals what works.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Real-world inspired experiments and micro-societies

- **Walden Two**  
  A planned experimental community based on behavioral science. It’s optimistic about design—but also raises the question of **who designs the designers**.
- **Island**  
  A counterpoint to *Brave New World*: a small society trying to deliberately get things right through conscious design and experimentation.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Tech / future-oriented “sandbox societies”

- **Balaji Srinivasan – *The Network State***  
  Proposes digitally organized communities that can eventually acquire territory and operate as new kinds of states—essentially **opt-in societies competing with each other**.
- **Seasteading Institute**  
  Advocates building floating communities to test new governance systems with minimal interference—very close to your “local experiments” constraint.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## A few hard problems these works keep circling

Your idea is coherent, but these authors tend to hit the same friction points:

- **Containment isn’t trivial**  
  Even “local” experiments can create spillovers (migration, environment, conflict). That’s why most stories introduce borders, exit rights, or oversight.
- **Consent and selection**  
  Who are the 150 people? Volunteers? Children born into it? This becomes ethically central very quickly.
- **Exit vs. lock-in**  
  Many systems look good initially but become oppressive if people can’t leave. So mobility becomes a key design feature.
- **Time horizon problem**  
  As you said, some ideas only prove themselves over generations—but people living through the experiment bear the cost.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## A useful way to frame your idea

What you’re describing is often summarized as:

> **“Let many systems compete in parallel, and let reality select the winners.”**

It’s a powerful idea—but most serious treatments add a constraint:

> **“…while ensuring people can exit losing systems before the costs become irreversible.”**

That’s where it becomes less about pure experimentation and more about **designing a safe experimental framework**.
