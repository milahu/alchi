## Response:

Yes—there *is* a substantial research tradition linking personality traits to how people deal with ambiguity. But the picture is a bit different (and more nuanced) than the Jung/MBTI “judging vs. perceiving” story you’re pointing to.

Below is a synthesis of what empirical psychology actually shows.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

1)  The core construct: “intolerance of ambiguity”  
    =================================================

The idea you’re describing has been studied for decades under names like:

- **intolerance of ambiguity**
- **ambiguity tolerance**
- **need for closure**
- **uncertainty avoidance**

Classic work (e.g., by Else Frenkel-Brunswik) defined ambiguity-intolerant individuals as those who:

- prefer **clear, black–white answers**
- **rush to closure**
- avoid incomplete or uncertain information [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4411993/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Later operationalizations explicitly measured:

- **speed of closure**
- **tendency to generalize from little data** [ETS](https://www.ets.org/research/policy_research_reports/publications/report/1959/hokb.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

👉 That already maps quite closely to your “quick but possibly wrong vs. slow but thorough” intuition.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

2)  Strong empirical links to modern personality traits (Big Five)  
    =================================================================

### (a) Openness to Experience (very robust)

- **Negative correlation with ambiguity intolerance**
- High openness → more comfort with uncertainty, novelty, complexity

Evidence:

- Ambiguity tolerance is linked to **originality and openness to ideas** [SpringerLink](https://bmcprimcare.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1471-2296-15-6?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- Studies explicitly compare the two constructs and show they are related but not identical [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0191886909001123?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

👉 This is probably the closest scientifically supported analogue to your “perceiving” style.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### (b) Conscientiousness (more mixed but relevant)

- Often linked to:
  - **preference for structure**
  - **goal-directed closure**
- Conceptually overlaps with:
  - “need for order”
  - “decisiveness”

This is the trait most similar to your “judging” idea—but:

- it does **not necessarily imply impulsivity or error-proneness**
- high conscientiousness can also mean **careful, disciplined thinking**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### (c) Agreeableness (weaker, but interesting)

- Some studies find:
  - **ambiguity aversion negatively correlated with agreeableness** [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4318272/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Interpretation:

- less tolerant of ambiguity → more rigid judgments of others

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### (d) Cognitive style traits (very close to your framing)

A key construct here is **Need for Closure** (Kruglanski):

- desire for **quick, definite answers**
- discomfort with ambiguity
- tendency to:
  - “seize” on early explanations
  - “freeze” on them (resist revision)

This is almost a direct empirical version of your:

> “quick fix vs. prolonged analysis” distinction

------------------------------------------------------------------------

3)  Behavioral and cognitive consequences (well established)  
    ===========================================================

High ambiguity intolerance is associated with:

- **premature closure / snap judgments** [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4411993/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- **mental rigidity** and lower flexibility [SpringerLink](https://bmcprimcare.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1471-2296-15-6?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- **stereotyping and prejudice** [SpringerLink](https://bmcprimcare.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1471-2296-15-6?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- preference for **certainty over accuracy**

Low ambiguity intolerance (i.e., high tolerance):

- more **exploratory thinking**
- greater **creativity** [tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10400419.2013.783762?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
- more willingness to delay decisions

------------------------------------------------------------------------

4)  Relation to Jung / MBTI “Judging vs Perceiving”  
    ==================================================

Here’s the critical reality check:

- The MBTI dichotomy (J vs P) is **not strongly supported in mainstream personality science**
- Modern research prefers **continuous traits** (Big Five, etc.)

However, conceptually:

| Your framing | Research equivalent |
|----|----|
| “Judging = fast closure, decisive” | **Need for closure**, low ambiguity tolerance, higher conscientiousness |
| “Perceiving = exploratory, open-ended” | **High openness**, ambiguity tolerance |

So your intuition is **psychologically meaningful**, but:

- it’s **not cleanly captured by MBTI categories**
- it is better explained as a **combination of multiple traits**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

5)  Important nuance (often overlooked)  
    ======================================

Your description contains a subtle assumption:

> “Quick = wrong, slow = better”

Research does **not support that as a general rule**.

Instead:

- High need for closure → faster decisions, **sometimes biased**
- But:
  - can be **efficient in stable environments**
- High ambiguity tolerance → deeper processing, but:
  - can lead to **indecision or over-analysis**

So it’s more like a **trade-off**:

| Style               | Strength                        | Weakness              |
|---------------------|---------------------------------|-----------------------|
| Fast closure        | efficiency, decisiveness        | bias, rigidity        |
| Ambiguity tolerance | flexibility, accuracy potential | slower, less decisive |

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Bottom line

- Yes—there is **strong empirical support** for personality differences in “avoidance of ambiguity.”
- Your intuition maps well onto real constructs:
  - **intolerance of ambiguity**
  - **need for closure**
  - **openness to experience**
- But:
  - The **MBTI “judging vs perceiving” dichotomy is not the scientific framework** used to study it
  - The phenomenon is better understood as **continuous traits + cognitive styles**, not binary types
