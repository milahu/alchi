You're thinking along the right lines. There are actually **two separate stresses** during a saltwater flood:

1.  **Flooding itself** (roots deprived of oxygen).
2.  **Salt left behind in the soil** after the water recedes.

Many plants can survive one of these stresses but not the other.

### Why salt is such a problem

Salt harms plants in several ways:

- It makes it harder for roots to absorb water (the soil can be wet, but physiologically "dry").
- Sodium and chloride ions can become toxic inside plant tissues.
- Salt disrupts nutrient uptake (potassium, calcium, etc.).
- Salt can damage soil structure, especially in clay soils.

After a major coastal flood or storm surge, the soil may remain salty for months or years unless rainfall or management washes the salts away.

## Are grasses and weeds generally more tolerant than crops?

Often yes, but not always.

Many wild plants have evolved under harsh conditions, while crops have been bred primarily for yield.

For example:

| Plant              | Salt tolerance            |
|--------------------|---------------------------|
| Mangroves          | Extremely high            |
| Salt-marsh grasses | Extremely high            |
| Saltbushes         | Very high                 |
| Barley             | Moderate                  |
| Wheat              | Moderate to low           |
| Rice               | Generally low to moderate |
| Corn (maize)       | Low                       |
| Soybean            | Low                       |

Interestingly, **barley** is one of the more salt-tolerant major cereal crops and is often used where salinity is a problem.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Are "primitive" plants more tolerant?

Not necessarily.

Evolution doesn't have a direction toward being "advanced." A flowering plant that evolved in a salt marsh may be far more salt-tolerant than a more ancient lineage.

For example:

- Many mosses are quite sensitive to salt.
- Many ferns are sensitive to salt.
- Some highly evolved flowering plants (halophytes) can grow in conditions approaching seawater salinity.

Salt tolerance depends much more on **specific adaptations** than on whether a plant lineage is ancient or recent.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## What happens after a coastal flood?

A rough ranking of survival chances:

1.  **Most vegetables and garden plants** → often die.
2.  **Corn, beans, many crops** → frequently suffer severe damage.
3.  **Some pasture grasses** → may recover after salts are washed out.
4.  **Salt-tolerant crops** (especially barley) → better chance.
5.  **Halophytes** (salt marsh plants, saltbushes, mangroves) → often survive or even benefit.

In severe seawater inundation, it is common for existing vegetation to die and for halophytes to colonize the area until rainfall gradually reduces soil salinity.

One interesting example is that after major storm surges and tsunamis, abandoned agricultural fields often temporarily transform into ecosystems dominated by salt-marsh plants, essentially reverting toward the type of vegetation that occupied those coastal areas before they were drained and farmed.

This gets into an important distinction:

**Plants generally cannot remove most of the salt from a soil by themselves.** The overwhelming majority of salt leaves through **leaching** (rainfall, flooding by fresh water, irrigation with drainage), not through plant harvest.

However, some plants can help **accelerate recovery** and a few can physically export salt when harvested.

## The best "salt-removing" forage plants

Among the forage halophytes discussed earlier, **saltbushes (Atriplex)** are probably the most effective.

### Saltbush (Atriplex)

Atriplex nummularia

Many Atriplex species:

- absorb large amounts of sodium and chloride,
- store salt in leaves,
- sometimes excrete salt through specialized structures.

If livestock eat the plant, much of the salt is redistributed through manure and urine elsewhere. If the plants are harvested and physically removed from the field, some salt leaves the site entirely.

This is called **phytoextraction**.

The limitation is scale:

- A flood may deposit tens of tonnes of salt per hectare.
- A crop of saltbush might remove only a fraction of that amount.

So it helps, but it is not a miracle cure.

## What actually removes the salt?

The dominant process is:

1.  Flood deposits salt.
2.  Salt-tolerant plants establish.
3.  Rainwater dissolves the salt.
4.  Drainage carries the salt downward or away.

Without steps 3 and 4, recovery is very slow.

A useful rule of thumb is that **one meter of rainfall can move vastly more salt through the soil profile than a year's worth of plant growth can remove in harvested biomass**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## A practical restoration sequence

After a coastal saltwater flood:

**Phase 1 (high salinity)**

- Glasswort (Salicornia)
- Seablite (Suaeda)
- Saltbush (Atriplex)

These survive the worst conditions and begin accumulating some salt.

**Phase 2 (moderate salinity)**

- Saltgrass (Distichlis)
- Salt-tolerant pasture grasses

As rainfall leaches salts away, these become more competitive.

**Phase 3 (lower salinity)**

- Conventional pasture species
- Clover
- Alfalfa
- More productive forage systems

This is roughly how many naturally recovering coastal landscapes transition after severe seawater intrusion.

If your goal is specifically **"what plant gives the most livestock forage while also helping reclaim saline soil?"**, saltbush (Atriplex) is probably the strongest overall candidate. It is one of the few plants that scores highly on all three criteria:

- survives salty soils,
- is useful ruminant forage,
- stores enough salt in its tissues to contribute meaningfully to phytoextraction when biomass is removed.

## Trees that tolerate salty soils

These generally prefer their roots not to be submerged in seawater continuously, but they can handle substantial salinity.

### Tamarisk

Tamarix

One of the most salt-tolerant non-mangrove trees.

Advantages:

- survives highly saline groundwater,
- excretes salt through leaf glands,
- grows in arid saline regions.

Disadvantages:

- invasive in many regions,
- not generally considered good livestock forage.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Australian saltbush relatives

Some tree-like members of the genus Atriplex can reach several meters in height, though botanically many are large shrubs rather than true trees.

These are often more useful for grazing than actual trees.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Casuarina

Casuarina equisetifolia

A common coastal tree.

Tolerates:

- sea spray,
- sandy coastal soils,
- moderate salinity.

Used extensively for coastal windbreaks.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Buttonwood

Conocarpus erectus

Often found behind mangrove zones.

Very tolerant of:

- saline soils,
- salt spray,
- occasional flooding.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Sea grape

Coccoloba uvifera

Common on tropical coastlines.

Can survive:

- strong salt spray,
- sandy soils,
- moderate salinity.

Produces edible fruit.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Olive

Olea europaea

Surprisingly salt tolerant compared with most fruit trees.

Not a halophyte, but considerably tougher than:

- apples,
- pears,
- peaches,
- cherries.

This is one reason olives thrive around the Mediterranean.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Date palm

Phoenix dactylifera

Remarkably tolerant of salinity.

Can survive irrigation water that would damage many other fruit crops.

Historically important in saline oasis agriculture.

## Trees that are useful for ruminants

If you're interested in a flood-resistant grazing system, some trees provide browse (leaves eaten by livestock).

Examples:

- Avicennia marina - browsed by camels, goats, and some wild herbivores.
- Atriplex nummularia - technically a shrub, but often managed like a small tree.
- Prosopis juliflora - salt tolerant and produces edible pods.
- Prosopis cineraria - important desert fodder tree.
- Acacia ampliceps - planted in saline rangelands.

## If your requirement is "survive a storm surge and still be useful afterward"

A rough ranking:

| Plant type          | Saltwater flood tolerance | Livestock value             |
|---------------------|---------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Mangroves           | Extremely high            | Low-moderate                |
| Saltbush (Atriplex) | Very high                 | Excellent                   |
| Tamarisk            | Very high                 | Poor                        |
| Date palm           | Moderate-high             | Moderate (fruit/byproducts) |
| Olive               | Moderate                  | Low forage value            |
| Acacia ampliceps    | Moderate-high             | Good                        |
| Mesquite            | Moderate-high             | Good                        |

For a landscape intended to recover after periodic coastal flooding while still feeding ruminants, many land managers would actually favor **saltbush + salt-tolerant grasses + fodder trees such as acacias or mesquite**, rather than relying primarily on mangroves. Mangroves are unmatched at surviving seawater, but they are generally less productive as livestock feed than dedicated forage shrubs and fodder trees.
